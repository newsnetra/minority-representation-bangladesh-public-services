    Bangladesh’s minorities rise — but never in uniform

    Bangladesh’s religious minorities can — and do — ascend through courts, classrooms, and government offices, but the country’s armed forces remain off-limits.

    Tasneem Khalil, Subinoy Eron

    Published October 22, 2024

    For Bangladesh’s religious minorities, most portals to power seem open.

    In bustling offices across the country, minority officers shape decisions in the police and civil services. They deliver justice as judges and advocates in packed courtrooms, guide generations of students at prestigious universities, and shape public opinion through prominent roles in the media. Their presence in Bangladesh’s corridors of power paints a vibrant picture of inclusivity — rare in a South Asia often marred by communal fractures.

    But at the military’s doorstep, their rise abruptly halts.

    Fewer than one percent of Bangladeshi army officers and just over one percent of navy officers are non-Muslim, according to a first-of-its-kind analysis of confidential military records conducted by Netra News.

    These figures contrast sharply with the country’s other key levers of power — such as the central police force, civil service, Dhaka University faculty, and elite professional guilds-where religious minorities ascend at a level that mirrors and, at times, even exceeds their nine-percent share of the population. That level of visibility is rare for minorities in South Asia, where officer-level government positions are prized gateways to the elite and tend to be overwhelmingly dominated by majority groups.

    To compile these findings, Netra News sifted through a wide spectrum of official and confidential records. Researchers accessed around 8,000 names from past army and navy rosters, along with a restricted police directory of about 2,500 officers, as well as publicly available information on civil servants, lower court judges, university faculty, and membership lists of two professional bodies.

    In total, the study mapped the likely religious affiliations of more than 40,000 individuals across Bangladesh and India, using both conventional methods and machine-learning models to classify identities.

    The higher up, the fewer

    Outside the barracks, minority inclusion in Bangladesh’s public institutions and professional societies often exceeds the national make-up of religious and ethnic minorities.

    More than 10% of lower court judges, 9% of police officers, and 10.5% of civil servants come from minority communities, with Hindus making up the largest share. Indigenous, Christian, and Buddhist representation is also strong.

    Non-Muslims represent over 10 percent of Dhaka University’s permanent faculty, 8.57 percent of members of the Dhaka Reporters Unity, and about 8 percent of the Supreme Court Bar Association — ratios that broadly reflect the national religious landscape.

    But gaps emerge — and widen — at higher ranks.

    At Dhaka University, 93 percent of top professorial posts are held by Muslims, slightly above their 91-percent share of the national population. In the lower judiciary, 92 percent of senior district judges and equivalents are Muslim.

    The disparity intensifies in the civil administration, where only one out of 74 top-tier secretaries identifies as non-Muslim. Police leadership posts mirror this pattern: just five of the 81 deputy inspector generals or higher are from minority communities.

    In the armed forces, however, the gap turns into a chasm.

    In the 2016 army roster obtained by Netra News, the highest-ranked non-Muslim officer — a brigadier general from the engineering corps — has since retired. In the 2017 navy rolls, there was just one non-Muslim commodore, also no longer serving.

    Beyond these two individuals, the records show 445 Muslim officers at equivalent or higher ranks, indicating that for every non-Muslim at the top, there were over 200 Muslim officers. In contrast, Bangladesh’s national population counts one non-Muslim among 12 citizens.

    Equally telling is the breakdown within this small minority.

    According to the data analysed by Netra News, there are no indigenous (Adivasi) personnel in the navy officer corps, just one Christian officer, and three Buddhists. In the army, no Christians at all appear in the officer ranks, while only seven indigenous officers and seven Buddhists were identified.

    The armed forces in Bangladesh recruit through a distinctive process through the Inter Services Selection Board (ISSB), which assesses candidates on personality, family background, and other factors — criteria that can include religion. Unlike many other government agencies, the military does not reserve quotas for marginalized communities or descendants of freedom fighters.

    Officially, the system is based on merit, yet the end result is a near-total absence of minorities in the upper echelons of military command.

    A Regional Perspective

    Bangladesh’s overall record on minority inclusion far surpasses that of its neighbors.

    Bangladesh’s military numbers echo India’s when it comes to uniformed minorities. In India, the largest minority group, Muslims, reportedly makes up only about two percent of the armed forces’ officer corps, even though they form around 14 percent of the national population — nearly double the share of Hindus in Bangladesh.

    Thus, the army and navy figures in Bangladesh, for all their stark shortfalls, are not entirely out of step with regional norms for religious representation in military ranks.

    However, Bangladesh far outperforms India in other arenas of governance.

    In Bangladesh’s civil service, around nine percent of officers are non-indigenous Hindus — 112 percent of their population share. In India, by contrast, Muslims comprise about 4.3 percent of the elite civil service, data shows. That’s just 28 percent of their national proportion.

    Put differently, adjusted for each country’s religious demographics, Hindus in Bangladesh’s civil service are nearly four times better represented than Muslims in India’s.

    Policing follows a similar pattern, with Hindus in Bangladesh reaching about 95 percent of their demographic share in the force, while Indian Muslims fill barely 28 percent of theirs.

    Bangladesh’s southern neighbor Nepal has its own intricacies.

    Official data suggests that Hindus, constituting 81 percent of Nepal’s population, occupy more than 90 percent of civil service positions.

    Nepal’s Muslim minority, conversely, accounts for over four percent of the population but just 0.1 percent of civil service posts, according to a 2016 study by Tek Bahadur Dong, a Nepalese social scientist with Social Science Baha.

    Indigenous Buddhist-majority groups beyond the Newar community — who together comprise over 30 percent of Nepal’s population — are massively underrepresented at only 1.6 percent of officer-level posts.

    Meanwhile, Bahuns (upper-caste Brahmins), 12 percent of the population, hold more than 70 percent of these coveted roles.

    Political minefield

    In Bangladesh, a civil service position is a highly coveted badge of social and economic status, a legacy of British colonial bureaucracy. Mass protests in July and August last year, ignited by accusations of bias in public-sector appointments, paved the way for the downfall of Prime Minister Sheikh Hasina’s long-standing administration.

    Conservative critics of the former Awami League government long claimed it had favored Hindu officers due to their perceived electoral loyalty.

    Yet the Netra News data suggests these complaints may be exaggerated.

    Minority representation in government agencies appears largely in line with — or slightly above — demographic expectations. If any institution stands apart, it is the armed forces, where the numbers of non-Muslim officers plummet dramatically.

    Despite the controversies, Bangladesh presents a complex tableau of inclusion: a setting where minority voices resonate in the courts, the cabinet secretariat, and academia, but still echo faintly in the military barracks.

    How Netra News reported this story

    Netra News obtained and reviewed 2016-17 restricted roasters of army, navy and police records, detailing a list of thousands of officers, alongside their ranks, joining dates and other crucial details. While the data may not reflect today’s force composition, the forces have announced no policy changes that would address the disparity in recruitment of religious minorities.

    We used Google Pinpoint and Tabula to extract entries from scanned PDF files to plain text in spreadsheets. We then manually fixed spelling errors and other inaccuracies in names and other details.

    Netra News also scraped public databases of officers in public administration, faculty members of Dhaka University, web directories of all of the 172 subordinate courts of Bangladesh, and membership lists of Supreme Court Bar Association and Dhaka Reporters Unity.

    Some lists contained only English transliterations. We used Open AI’s API and Google Translate API to convert them to Bangla transliterations, some of which were manually fixed. The Supreme Court Bar Association’s membership list contained members’ religious background.

    We obtained a list of common Hindu and Indigenous surnames from four Wikipedia pages, and added the rest ourselves.

    We then developed a cleaning technique to assign likely religion based on their surname:

    =ARRAYFORMULA(TEXTJOIN(", ", TRUE, IF(LEN(A2), IFERROR(VLOOKUP(FILTER(SPLIT(A2, " "), SPLIT(A2, " ") <> ""), 'Sheet with Non-Muslim surnames'!A:B, 2, FALSE), ""), "")))

    If A2 contains the name, the formula will divide the names into several pieces. If A2 contains "মৃণাল কান্তি দাস”, it will first divide it into three parts: “মৃণাল”, “কান্তি” and “দাস” and match each of the parts with the list of surnames to see if they match with any surname. For matches, it will return the respective likely religion name(s). Since both কান্তি and দাস are considered traditionally Hindu names, the formula will return “হিন্দু, হিন্দু”. In a column next, we also return the exact surnames that are matches. In the above example, it will return “কান্তি” and “দাস”. For such multiple matches, we considered them to be safer determination. For the rest, we manually checked over to see if any mistake occurred.

    However, some surnames are common among Muslim, Hindus and even Indigenous communities, such as Chowdhury (চৌধুরী) and Dewan (দেওয়ান). In such cases, we considered the first name and in other cases, their parents’ names, when available.

    The number of indigenous officers may be undercounted because some communities follow Christian and Hindu norms for naming themselves. Our list of traditionally non-Muslim surnames can be found here.

    Bangladesh’s 2022 census says the country has 1% indigenous people in its 165 million population. We estimate 60% of them to follow Buddhist, 20% to follow Hinduism and 15% to follow Christianity. Accordingly, to find the share of indigenous irrespective of their religions in population, we deducted the similar share from total Hindu, Buddhist and Christian populations to find a national share of all religions.

    To find the share of officers by likely religion among Indian Administrative Service and Indian Police Service officer corps, we obtained their latest available public list. We employed two machine-learning-based predictive models: “It is all in the name” developed by researchers at University of Illinois Chicago and University of Sussex, and “pranaam”, a Python library that uses 4 million unique records of Indian names and their religions. Both have a very high accuracy rate, and they agreed on the likely religion of the officers’ names in more than 90% of the cases. We manually determined some of the cases through reporting and research, while discarded the rest. We analyzed the names of more than 9,000 Indian officers.
